Gorilla Trekking
Gorilla tracking and habituation
A). Gorilla tracking

Mountain gorilla trekking stands as Uganda's premier tourist attraction, drawing significant numbers of visitors to experience these magnificent primates in their natural habitat. The thick forests of Bwindi Impenetrable National Park and Mgahinga Gorilla National Park are home to half of the world's surviving mountain gorillas, where this remarkable activity of tracking gorillas occurs.

Participants embark on guided hikes through unspoiled tropical forests, encountering these incredible creatures in their natural habitat. It is an incredibly thrilling experience to see these gentle giants up close while observing their actions, interactions, and the magnificent beauty of their natural setting.

Gorilla tracking takes several hours as participants traverse steep hills, navigating thick vegetation in the search to observe these majestic and gentle creatures in their native environment. The trek takes participants through challenging terrains, culminating in a remarkable encounter with the tranquil gorillas amidst the lush greenery of the forest. The incredible moment of coming face-to-face with these magnificent creatures creates a lasting and memorable experience for those fortunate enough to undertake the trek.

Gorilla tracking permits all participants to spend one hour close to the designated gorilla family. This deliberate limitation minimizes the time humans spend close to gorillas while preserving the forest's natural splendor.

Tracking mountain gorillas in Uganda requires obtaining a permit from the Uganda Wildlife Authority at least three (or more) months before the tracking date through a locally licensed tour company. Securing a gorilla tracking permit earlier enables guests to track gorillas on the preferred dates. At Samora Africa Safaris, we are here to assist you in securing your gorilla tracking permit and any other services you may need. Kindly contact us, and we organize your safari to your satisfaction.

Age restriction

The minimum age limit for gorilla tracking in Uganda is 15 years old and above

Notable gorilla families in Bwindi consist of the following:

Gorilla tracking in Bwindi Impenetrable National Park takes place in two distinct sectors: the northern and southern regions. Each sector features two tracking locations where visitors can see different gorilla families. In the northern sector, tracking occurs from Buhoma and Ruhija, while the southern sector offers tracking from Rushaga and Nkuringo.

Across the four tracking locations, approximately 24 habituated gorilla families are available for visits each day. To support conservation efforts, a maximum of eight visitors track each gorilla family daily.

1. Rushaga

Rushaga tracking location has 11 habituated gorilla families, which is the highest number of gorilla families in Bwindi Impenetrable National Park. It attracts a high number of visitors to the park daily compared to the other locations. Nshongi, Mishaya, Bweza, Bikyingi, Kahungye, Busingye, Mucunguzi, Rwigi, and Kutu gorilla family groups are a few examples of gorilla families.

2. Nkuringo

Like Rushaga, the southern sector of Bwindi hosts the Nkuringo tracking location and has four habituated gorilla families tracked daily. They include Posho, Bushaho, Nkuringo, and Christmas.

3. Buhoma

Buhoma, located in Bwindi's northern sector, has been the most visited location compared to other Bwindi sectors. The tracking of mountain gorillas began at Buhoma in 1993, with the Mubare gorilla family being the first to become habituated. Buhoma houses the offices of Bwindi Impenetrable National Park Headquarters, making it the most significant site. Currently, Buhoma tracks seven habituated families, including Mubare, Habinyanja, Katwe, Rushegura, Muyambi, and Bunyindo, daily.

4. Ruhija

Ruhija tracking site is in the northern sector of Bwindi Forest, where only four habituated gorilla families are tracked daily. Ruhija stands at 2,350 meters above sea level, which makes it the highest and coldest part of Bwindi. The four (04) habituated gorilla families in Ruhija include Kyaguriro, Oruzugo, Bitukura, and Mukiza.

B). Gorilla Habituation

Gorilla habituation is the step-by-step process of preparing wild mountain gorillas to become comfortable with the presence of humans. The process involves researchers and conservation activists taking a considerable amount of time, months, or years to make gorillas slowly get used to human presence without stressing them or disrupting their normal behavior.

For conservation purposes, a limited number of visitors participate in this exceptional and informative session of gorilla habituation.

In contrast to ordinary gorilla trekking, where groups have already become used to and acclimated to the presence of humans, gorilla habituation experiences provide guests with a unique chance to observe the habituation process, enabling them to have a more profound understanding of the lives of these critically endangered primates.

Differences between gorilla tracking and habituation

Much like regular gorilla tracking provides an impactful overview of gorillas' lives, habituation allows for a more extended and thorough observation, providing a greater insight into the gorillas' daily activities.

Unlike gorilla tracking, that begins at 8:00 am, gorilla habituation starts earlier at 6:30 am and continues until participants return to the briefing point for debriefing.

The viewing time differs between gorilla tracking and gorilla habituation once you find the gorillas. While gorilla tracking permits a maximum of one hour with the gorillas, gorilla habituation allows up to four hours of interaction.

Gorilla habituation costs about twice as much as regular gorilla tracking. It is because it's a more informed and detailed process. Currently, gorilla habituation costs 1500 USD per person for foreign nonresidents (FNR) and 1000 USD for foreign residents (FR). Usual gorilla tracking costs 800 USD for foreign nonresidents (FNR) and 700 USD for foreign residents (FR).

Gorilla tracking rules in Uganda

Mountain gorilla tracking adheres to several rules. These strict rules are essential for preventing human-gorilla disease transmission and preserving gorillas for future generations. Typically, visitors are briefed before their gorilla encounter to ensure they fully understand these rules.

The major rules include the following:

Visitors must be free from any illnesses that could infect the gorillas. Those with communicable diseases are likely to have their participation prohibited.

Keep a minimum distance of 7 meters (21 feet) from the mountain gorillas to reduce the possibility of disease transmission and to prevent upsetting the gorillas' normal behavior.

The Uganda Wildlife Authority strictly prohibits flashing cameras because they may frighten gorillas. It is advisable for participants to use flash-free cameras and refrain from making abrupt movements.

Maintain a low volume of noise. Loud noises may cause anger and disrupt the gorillas' routine.

To avoid any possible encounter, eating and drinking are prohibited when in the vicinity of the gorillas.

To protect the ecosystem, dump all waste, such as used tissues, in the designated trash cans outside the forest.

Guests must follow the guidelines provided by the professional guides and rangers who trek with the groups. The guidelines include instructions on behavior, motion, and safety measures.

Despite the gorillas' acclimatization to human presence, avoid direct eye contact. They might interpret direct eye contact as a threat to their communication.

Gorilla tracking requirements

For a smooth and successful gorilla-tracking adventure, participants must have the following requirements:

Participants should wear hiking boots during the gorilla trekking expedition to avoid falls since the trails become slippery when it rains.

Wear long-sleeved shirts, tops, and pants to protect yourself from insects and prickly plants.

Use an Insect repellant. The forest is home to a variety of insects, and the use of insect repellant reduces insect bites.

Carry a rain jacket because the weather in Bwindi is unpredictable. It can rain at any time of the day during the gorilla tracking adventure.

Waterproof pack covers to protect your phone or camera if it rains during gorilla trekking.

Carry a camera to capture memorable moments. However, a cell phone with a high-resolution camera can be a good substitute.

White Water Rafting
White water rafting

Water rafting is a recreational activity in which participants steer an inflatable raft down a river or other waterways.

In Uganda, whitewater rafting is among the adventure sports that attract visitors. It occurs on the iconic Nile River because of its varying grades of water rapids.

During rafting, participants have a choice to enjoy the various grades. They can opt for either grade 3 or the extreme grade 5. The activity takes several hours, offering adventure enthusiasts a chance to enjoy it to the maximum.

Rafting adventures on the Nile River typically involve groups traversing a 21-kilometer route that combines calm waters and those with varying classes of rapids. This activity is available any day throughout the year.

Game Viewing

Game viewing

Game viewing is a major tourism activity in Uganda, attracting visitors from across the world. The country has numerous savannah game parks and reserves, home to a diverse array of wildlife, including lions, giraffes, buffaloes, antelopes, leopards, elephants, and more.

A game drive is an exciting safari experience where you explore wildlife parks in open-roof 4x4 vehicles, often with an armed ranger as your guide. The game parks have well-maintained trails that traverse through several habitats, each presenting a different viewpoint. It's worth noting that the best time to enjoy thrilling game drives is in the morning or evening, as this allows for better encounters with various animals when they are actively grazing.

In Uganda, top destinations for thrilling wildlife viewing are the savannah game parks and wildlife reserves. These areas showcase intricate ecosystems sustaining remarkable species and highlight Uganda's biodiversity.

The most premium destinations for game viewing in Uganda, among others, include the following:

A). Murchison Falls National Park

Murchison Falls National Park is the largest protected area in Uganda, renowned for its rich and abundant wildlife and the natural beauty of the winding River Nile. The park derives its name from the Murchison Falls, a remarkable spot along the River Nile where water cascades 43 meters into a narrow gorge approximately 7 meters wide.

The Nile River divides Murchison Falls National Park into two distinct ecosystems: the northern and southern regions. The northern sector consists of open savannah grasslands supporting diverse wildlife. The southern area boasts woodlands that provide habitat for primates and numerous bird species.

The park is home to a wide variety of wildlife, including 451 bird species and over 76 animal species. Buffaloes, Jackson hartebeests, antelopes, lions, giraffes, hyenas, and warthogs are among the varied residents. The park's topography deeply embeds these animals, allowing them to traverse the Savannah grasslands for grazing or hunting.

The plains of the northern part of Murchison Falls National Park are relatively flat, ideal for game viewing, and offer an immersive experience with the treasures of the animal kingdom in a natural and protected setting.

Besides the usual game drives in 4x4 safari vehicles, Murchison Falls National Park also offers boat excursions that allow visitors to see diverse wildlife along the banks of the Nile River. During the boat trips, visitors may see aquatic wildlife, including crocodiles and hippos, and territorial species that come to the riverbanks to hydrate and cool off from the intense heat.

B). Queen Elizabeth National Park

Queen Elizabeth National Park is in the Kasese district of Western Uganda and covers approximately 1,978 square kilometers. When established in 1952, the park was initially known as Kazinga National Park. It was renamed in 1954 after a visit to the park by Queen Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom. The park is on the rift valley floor, providing stunning views of the escarpments, crater lakes, and the diverse ranges of the Rwenzori Mountain.

In Queen Elizabeth National Park, there are two main areas where you can see wildlife: the northern and the southern sectors. First, the northern section encompasses the Kasenyi and north Kazinga plains. This area is home to many species of animals, such as leopards, lions, elephants, buffaloes, and more. The southern sector, also known as the Ishasha sector, offers guests exceptional opportunities to observe tree-climbing lions and other species, including Topis.

In addition to the usual game drives in 4x4 safari vehicles, Queen Elizabeth National Park offers boat trips along the Kazinga Channel, allowing visitors to see numerous water-adapted animals and birds along the channel's banks. The Kazinga Channel is a 32-kilometer freshwater link that connects Lake Edward and Lake George within the park. The channel attracts a wide variety of animals and birds. Notable inhabitants include several Nile crocodiles and one of the massive concentrations of hippos in the world.

Experiential Tourism in Queen Elizabeth National Park

Experiential tourism involves using scientific gadgets to track the animals. Through active participation, participants in this unique activity can learn about the general behaviors, group dynamics, and makeup of the observed animals from experienced professionals.

Only Queen Elizabeth National Park in Uganda allows experiential tourism, which involves tracking lions, banding mongooses, and censuring hippos.

C). Kidepo Valley National Park

Kidepo Valley National Park, located in the northeast part of Uganda, is generally considered the most isolated park in the country. The park is a hidden treasure for those who love wildlife and want to experience unparalleled game-viewing opportunities.

The park is best known for being one of the few places in Uganda where one can experience wildlife in its purest form. It differs from other parks in Uganda because it still has many rare and endangered animal species. This uniqueness makes the park a must-visit destination for those looking to experience nature in its most virgin state.

Kidepo Valley National Park boasts numerous species of wild animals, including buffaloes, African bush elephants, lions, cheetahs, different types of antelopes, leopards, giraffes, jackals, and hyenas. A game drive within this park will guarantee an incredible and rewarding encounter with most of these species.

D). Lake Mburo National Park

Lake Mburo National Park is the smallest game park in Uganda and features an array of species. The park hosts over sixty unique species of mammals, such as antelopes (Topis, Elands, Impalas, Reedbucks, Deffassa Waterbucks, and Oribis), hyenas, zebras, buffaloes, warthogs, hippos, and recently relocated giraffes, creating a harmonious ecosystem that captivates visitors with its natural beauty.

Beyond its terrestrial inhabitants, Lake Mburo National Park is also a vibrant habitat for many bird species. With an impressive count of 350 species, the park is a paradise for bird enthusiasts, featuring water birds, forest dwellers, and savanna birds. This avian diversity contributes to the park's allure, offering a captivating experience for those interested in the rich tapestry of Uganda's birdlife.

Lake Mburo National Park is generally where nature's beauty unfolds in an unaltered setting. Whether seeing the elegant giraffes in the distance or listening to the beautiful sounds of the park's birds, every moment within its confines guarantees an engaging and fascinating experience with the wonders of the animal kingdom.

Lake Mburo National Park is the only protected area in Uganda that offers a guided walking safari. During the 3-hour nature walk, you may closely encounter several animals and birds. On the walking safari, you can see antelopes (impalas, waterbucks, topis, elands, oribis, and bushbucks), giraffes, zebras, buffaloes, warthogs, and other animals up close.

Game viewing in Rwanda

In Rwanda, Akagera National Park is the exclusive destination for game viewing, as it is the country's sole savanna park. Situated in eastern Rwanda, the park is home to Africa's renowned "Big Five": lions, rhinos, elephants, leopards, and buffaloes. In addition to these iconic species, game drives in Akagera National Park offer opportunities to see other wildlife species, including zebras, antelopes, hippos, and giraffes.

Chimpanzee Tracking
Chimpanzee tracking

Besides mountain gorilla tracking, chimpanzee tracking is another popular and fascinating experience that involves participants walking into thick forests to observe chimpanzees as they swing from the tree branches, feed, and socialize.

Chimpanzees and humans share 98.7% of their DNA, which makes them the living closest cousins to human beings. Some of their other fascinating characteristics include their ability to smash nuts with stones, obtain water from empty pods, and remove termites from their burrows using sticks.

Chimpanzee tracking in Uganda

Uganda offers numerous locations for tracking habituated chimpanzees in their natural habitats, all designated as national parks or forest reserves. These include Kibale Forest National Park, Budongo Forest, Kalinzu Forest Reserve, Kyambura Gorge, and Bugoma Forest, to mention but a few. Tracking chimpanzees in Uganda offers exceptional and unforgettable experiences that allow participants a close encounter with these fascinating and brilliant primates.

The most prominent location for tracking chimpanzees is Kibale Forest National Park. The park is home to more than 1500 chimpanzees, and this significant number offers guests a high chance of encountering these primates during guided tracking excursions.

Besides chimpanzees, Kibale Forest National Park is also home to several species of primates, birds, and butterflies. In addition to chimpanzee tracking and habituation, the park supports other activities such as nature walks and bird watching.

Kibale Forest National Park regulates chimpanzee tracking for conservation purposes, offering two daily sessions: morning and afternoon.

Tracking chimpanzees in Uganda requires obtaining a permit from the Uganda Wildlife Authority at least three months before the tracking date through a locally licensed tour company. Chimpanzee tracking in Rwanda

Tracking chimpanzees is one of the most popular activities in Rwanda. It’s exclusively conducted in the high-altitude rainforest known as Nyungwe Forest. Nyungwe Forest National Park covers an area of approximately 1,019 square kilometers in the southern part of Rwanda and is Africa’s largest protected mountain forest. It is home to a diverse array of bird and wildlife species, including approximately 500 chimpanzees. Visitors to the park track only two habituated troops out of the total number of chimpanzee individuals. The main forest is home to one troop, while Cyamudongo in the eastern part of Nyungwe Forest National Park is home to another.

The habituated group in the main forest has the highest number of chimpanzees, but tracking this group is quite challenging because of the rugged terrain. However, the Cyamudongo group, consisting of only 30 individual chimps, provides successful chimpanzee tracking experiences.

Age restriction for chimpanzee tracking in Rwanda

Similar to gorilla tracking, chimpanzee tracking also has age restrictions. Chimpanzee tracking in Rwanda restricts participation to individuals aged 15 years and above.

Requirements for chimpanzee tracking

Participants must have a tracking permit from the relevant authorization body through a local licensed tour company or agency.

Those tracking should wear hiking shoes, preferably boots that offer ankle support and grip, preventing slips, falls, and twists. Sturdy shoes also shield the feet from bites, stings, and cuts while walking through the forest.

Bird Watching

Uganda is widely regarded as one of the top destinations for bird-watching, attracting enthusiasts from around the globe. The country has diverse ecosystems, including rainforests, expansive savannahs, swamps, mountains, semi-arid deserts, and Afro-alpine zones, which provide sanctuary for over 1,000 avian species.

In Uganda, there are wide selections of prime birding spots, which provide opportunities to see a variety of bird species. These sites encompass distinguished forests like Bwindi, Kibale, Budongo, Mabira, Mpanga, and Kalinzu, serving as habitats for forest avifauna and Albertine endemics. Savannah parks like Kidepo, home to raptors, Murchison Falls, Queen Elizabeth, and Lake Mburo National Parks, offer unique environments for numerous bird species. Entebbe town and its surroundings are well-recognized birding destinations for forest and water birds.

The country’s attractiveness for bird-watching enthusiasts extends to other prominent areas, such as Mabamba Swamp, which is home to the distinctive prehistoric shoebill, and other notable birding sites like Semliki National Park and Ziwa Rhino Sanctuary. The existence of several birding spots illustrates the effort and dedication Uganda is taking to conserve the diverse ecosystems. The country reaffirms its position as a global center for bird-watching lovers seeking a unique and enriching adventure.

Bird watching in Rwanda

Rwanda offers several premier birding destinations that attract enthusiasts from around the world, providing opportunities to observe more than 700 avian species. Among these are 29 Albertine Rift endemics. Key birding sites include Nyungwe National Park and Volcanoes National Park, renowned for montane bird species, and Akagera National Park, which hosts numerous savannah and wetland birds.

Community Visits
Community visits

Uganda is home to diverse groups of people, each having distinct cultural beliefs and norms. The profound differences that underscore this distinctiveness of Ugandan cultures are evident in the numerous languages spoken, the norms, and the unique perspectives on national identity.

Ugandans can be categorized into three primary ethnic groups: the Bantu, the Nilotics, and the Nile Hamites, each having its own unique customs, traditions, and beliefs. Within these ethnic groups, significant cultural variations exist, shaped by historical and regional influences. Some tribes in Uganda have maintained their original traditions and norms, preserving the essence of their pre-colonial way of life. This preservation is evident in diverse aspects, including traditional attire, dance forms, and socio-economic interactions.

Visiting local communities is essential in enhancing the guests’ understanding of the diversity among Africans and breaking down misconceptions. To appreciate the beauty and diversity of the Ugandan people, we at Samora Africa Safaris include visits to some of the local communities close to the major national parks, such as the Batwa, Bahima, Karamojong, and Batoro.

The Batwa

The Batwa are former residents of the rainforests in the Virunga Mountains of Rwanda, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Uganda. They lived a simple life in the forests, hunting and food-gathering without engaging in farming. In Uganda, they lived in the Bwindi and Mgahinga forests alongside the mountain gorillas and other wildlife. The Ugandan government relocated the Batwa from the forests to preserve the flora and fauna, but they have maintained their traditional lifestyle.

A visit to the Batwa community will provide insight into the ancient era of the Pygmies before the agricultural revolution. They will demonstrate their traditional hunting skills, fire-making techniques, and dances.

The Karamojong

The Karamajong are nomadic pastoralists living in northeast Uganda, close to Kidepo Valley National Park. As traditional pastoralists, the Karamojong keep large numbers of cattle, and they take pride in their traditions and cultures.

Despite the current era of modernization, the Karamojong have vehemently refused to adapt to new lifestyles. They have persisted and jealously preserved their pre-colonial lifestyles, living communally as large extended families with numerous homesteads (Manyattas). Besides living communally in large homesteads, the Karamojong people physically stand out as unique from the rest of Ugandans due to their distinctive dressing style and noticeable body markings.

During a wildlife viewing safari in Kidepo Valley National Park, we include a visit to one of the homesteads within the park’s confines to give visitors a chance to engage with local elders and tour the kraals, see grinding stones, and other fascinating items. Additionally, visitors will observe their dress codes and the extent of their selfish affection for their cows.

The Batooro

The Batoro are part of the Bantu ethnic group that inhabits the Bigodi area, which is close to Kibale Forest National Park. The Bigodi community offers guests a unique glimpse into the daily lives and traditions of the locals, allowing visitors to appreciate their socio-economic practices.

The walk through the Bigodi community offers guests an opportunity to observe the everyday activities of the local population, making it an enriching safari experience. Participants in the Bigodi community walk have the opportunity to observe the traditional brew-making process, interact directly with the locals, and explore the green plantations surrounding the community.

The Bahima

The Bahima, who live around Lake Mburo in western Uganda, are pastoralists like the Karamojong. A visit to a Bahima homestead while visiting Lake Mburo National Park enhances your understanding of the community in general.

Hiking
Hiking

In contrast to mountain climbing, hiking typically takes place at lower elevations; the Virunga volcanic ranges, with their intermediate elevations each offering a unique and alluring experience, are one example.

Uganda has many hiking options at varying altitudes. Three mountains in southwestern Uganda offer hiking opportunities: Mount Mgahinga (347 meters), Mount Muhabura (4127 meters), and Mount Sabinyo (3644.5 meters).

Similar to Uganda, hiking takes place in two Virunga Mountains in Rwanda. The two volcanoes are Mount Bisoke and Mount Karisimbi, extending to altitudes of 3711 meters and 4507 meters, respectively. These options cater to enthusiasts and adventurers with diverse preferences, skills, and trekking experiences.

Mountain Climbing

Mountain climbing is one of the major tourism activities in the East African region. It’s a physically demanding activity that involves ascending and descending slopes along the already-established trails.

East Africa and Uganda particularly provide plenty of opportunities for mountain climbers in their attempt to conquer high altitudes and explore various landscapes and terrains. The mountains in this region have varying surfaces, including rocks, ice, and snow. Therefore, climbers must equip themselves with the necessary gear and tools. The mountains, with their varied elevations and peaks, offer a platform for individuals to test their endurance and abilities amidst the challenges of their terrain.

There are two mountains in Uganda that hikers can explore.

1. Mountain Rwenzori

The Rwenzori Mountain, sometimes called the Mountain of the Moon, is in the Kasese district of western Uganda at the border between the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda. The mountain comprises several ranges, stretching 20 km in length and 65 km in width.

The mountain has six massifs with varying heights, each separated by a gorge, offering climbers different hiking opportunities and challenges. The notable massifs include Mount Baker (4,843 meters), Mount Emin (4798 meters), Mount Stanley (5,18 meters), Mount Speke (4890 meters), Mount Gessi (4,715 meters), and Mount Luigi di Savoia (4,627 meters).

Climbers can select from several choices based on their hobbies and physical capabilities. However, it takes between eight and nine days to hike to Margherita, the highest peak. On this breathtaking ascent, trekkers encounter a range of vegetation zones, many of which closely correlate with the height. These zones are the Afro Montane Forest, which is between 1800 and 2500 meters; the Bamboo-Mimulopsis zone at 2500 and 3000 meters; the Heather-Rapanea zone at 3000 and 4000 meters; and, lastly, the Alpine zone, which is between 4000 and 4500 meters.

Exploring Mountain Rwenzori offers the chance to see and enjoy the variety of ecosystems that flourish at its varied altitudes.

2. Mount Elgon

Mount Elgon is an extinct volcano situated along the Uganda-Kenya border, renowned for its vast landscapes and distinctive fauna, providing climbers with the opportunity to experience the tranquil and less congested alpine environment.

The mountain rises to 4,321 meters above sea level, with Wagagai Peak being the highest point on the Ugandan side. With a circumference of almost 40 kilometers, it has one of the biggest undamaged calderas in the world, which draws geologists and intrepid travelers.

The slopes of Mount Elgon feature spectacular views comprising steep ridges, cliffs, valleys, waterfalls, and verdant plant zones that vary with elevation.

Due to its elevation, Mount Elgon experiences a cool and wet climate. The rainy seasons, from March to May and October to November, transform the landscape into lush greenery but can make trekking more challenging. In contrast, the dry seasons, from June to September and December to February, provide ideal conditions for outdoor activities, with clear skies and pleasant weather.

There are several hiking options available, each determined by the trekking trail chosen. These trails include Sasa, Piswa, and Sipi, all leading to Wagagai Peak.

Nature Walks

Nature walks are guided strolls through wetlands, woods, or wildlife reserves that provide learning and observation of the local flora and fauna. In Uganda, guided nature walks in private establishments or protected areas are essential for in-depth investigation as they provide people with an opportunity to create a connection with the natural world.

In Uganda, nature walks take place in the following protected areas:

A). Murchison Falls National Park

In Murchison Falls National Park, there are nature treks to the top of the falls. After the boat docks at the bottom of the falls, hikers ascend the hill to view the world's most powerful waterfall. The trek to the top of the falls typically takes thirty to thirty-five minutes.

There are several pathways in the Kaniyo Pabidi and Rabongo woods, where you might see primates and birds.

B). Queen Elizabeth National Park

Queen Elizabeth National Park offers nature walks throughout its many ecosystems. There are paths along Lake George, the Mweya Peninsula, the Katwe Salt Lake, and the Kyambura Gorge. Strolling along Lake George's edges increases your chances of spotting the elusive shoebill and the semi-aquatic Sitatunga antelope. In addition to trekking to witness traditional salt mining, Katwe Salt Lake is home to many primate species, including chimpanzees, in the Kyambura Gorge.

C). Virunga Mountains

There are hiking trails across the slopes of the Virunga Mountains, particularly on Mount Gahinga and Mount Muhabura. In addition to the stunning views of the surrounding lakes and rural communities, this adventure puts you near nature and increases your chances of observing uncommon bird species.

D). Kibale Forest National Park

There are well-maintained and developed nature walk pathways in Kibale National Park. Forest walks are flexible, lasting from three hours to shorter durations depending on your time and interests. You could see a variety of primates, animals, and forest-dwelling bird species while on the nature walks. For guests interested in spotting nocturnals such as bats, owls, pottos, civets, and bush babies, the park authorities allow a nighttime nature walk.

E). Bwindi Impenetrable National Park

In Bwindi Impenetrable National Park, there are several trails where nature walks can last many hours, offering the chance to observe a diverse range of animal, bird, butterfly, and primate species.

Sport Fishing

Sport fishing, a popular leisure activity in Uganda, attracts both local and international anglers using rods, reels, and various baits. Enthusiasts participate in this lively pursuit by boat on lakes or along shores, riverbanks, and private ponds.

Uganda has several fishing locations that provide plenty of alternatives for sport fishing:

The most significant locations for sport fishing include Lake Victoria and Lake Mburo. However, other notable places offer avenues for sport fishing excursions, such as resorts and hotels along Lake Victoria's shores in Entebbe, Jinja, Ssese Islands, and Kampala.

During sport fishing, participants enjoy an exceptional moment of fun, relaxation, and breathtaking views. This activity adds a recreational dimension to the East African safari experience, giving visitors a unique and unforgettable opportunity to interact with nature.

There are many fish species caught during sport fishing expeditions, including Nile Perch, Tilapia, Tiger Fish, Catfish, and Barbel (yellow fish).
